\section*{Abstract}
This project investigates Tube-Based Stochastic Model Predictive Control (SMPC) as a less conservative alternative to Robust Tube MPC for systems subject to stochastic disturbances and uncertainties. The team implements both controllers on a two-mass spring-damper system and introduce the SARAH-M variance-reduced stochastic gradient algorithm as the optimizer for the scenario-based MPC optimization problem. Constraint tightening for the stochastic method is handled probabilistically via Monte Carlo simulation of the closed-loop error dynamics rather than worst-case bounding like the robust method. Simulation results show that the stochastic tube is significantly tighter than the robust worst-case tube, at the cost of increased per-step computation time. We conclude that SARAH-M based SMPC is feasible for moderate real-time requirements and offers a practical trade-off between conservatism and computational complexity.

\section{Introduction}
Control systems are increasingly moving towards complex and uncertain environments.
Model Predictive Control (MPC) is the standard for constrained systems, but its standard deterministic form lacks a formal mechanism to handle perturbations.
Tube-Based Stochastic MPC (SMPC) represents a sophisticated evolution, providing a framework for maintaining stability within a defined "tube" of trajectories.

\subsection{Tube-Based MPC}
In tube-based MPC, the system state is decomposed into two distinct components: the nominal system, which is the controlled, deterministic model that ignores noise,
and the error system, which measures the deviation between the plant and the nominal model, caused by stochastic disturbances (perturbations) \cite{LANGSON2004125,dey2026adaptivetubempccommon}.
By centering an invariant set around the nominal trajectory, the controller ensures that as long as the nominal state is controlled effectively, the true state of the system is guaranteed to remain within the tube boundaries with a certain probability.

\subsection{Robust vs. Stochastic Tubes}
Traditional Robust Tube MPC assumes worst-case bounded disturbances, which often results in overly conservative control actions.\cite{dey2026adaptivetubempccommon}
Stochastic Tube MPC relaxes this in three ways:
first, by characterizing the probability distribution of the disturbances;
second, by shrinking the constraints based on a risk budget rather than the maximum possible perturbation;
and thirdly, by allowing the system to operate closer to its physical limits, improving the performance without sacrificing the safety margin \cite{smpcSurvey1,smpcSurvey2,smpcSurvey3}.

\subsection{Objective}
This project seeks to determine the feasibility of Tube-Based SMPC in practical applications.
First, the authors will outline the application of a Robust Tube-based MPC for a simple spring-damper system.
Next, the authors will implement a Stochastic Tube-based MPC using the SARAH-M algorithm \cite{YANG2024122295}. The SARAH-M algorithm is a variance-reduced stochastic gradient method, and in this project, it is used to solve a scenario-average cost at each time step \cite{doi:10.1137/1.9781611973433}. The contraint tightening margin for the stochastic method is derived from a Monte Carlo simulation of the closed-loop error dynamics rather than a worst-case bound margin that is used for the robust tube-based MPC. Finally, the two approaches are compared on trajectory tracking performance and per-step computation time.
Finally, the authors will compare the performance profile of the tube-based SMPC to a Robust Tube MPC.

\section{Technical Background}
\subsection{State-Space Equation}
\begin{figure}
    \centering    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{spring_damper_system.png}
    \label{spring-damper-system}
    \caption{Spring-damper system model.}
\end{figure}

Following the work of Haber\cite{haber2023mpc1}, the physical model of a two-spring, two-damper system is given by Fig.~\ref{spring-damper-system}.
Applying Newton's second law to each mass yields the equations of motion:
$$m_1 \ddot{x}_1 = -(k_1+k_2)x_1 - (d_1+d_2)\dot{x}_1 + k_2 x_2 + d_2 \dot{x}_2$$
$$m_2 \ddot{x}_2 = k_2 x_1 + d_2 \dot{x}_1 - k_2 x_2 - d_2 \dot{x}_2 + F$$

Choose state vector $\mathbf{x} = [x_1, \dot{x}_1, x_2, \dot{x}_2]^\top$, control input $u = F$, and output $y = x_1$.
Then the state-space equation for this system is given by:
$$\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A_c \mathbf{x} + B_c u, \qquad y = C \mathbf{x}$$
here the matrices $A_c$, $B_c$, and $C$ are populated directly from the physical parameters.

The system is then discretized using the forward Euler approximation:
$$A = (I - T_s A_c)^{-1}, \qquad B = A \cdot T_s B_c$$

\subsection{Model Predictive Control}
Model predictive control is an optimization-based control strategy that explicitly uses a dynamic model to predict future plant behavior over a finite prediction horizon $N_p$.
MPC chooses control inputs that minimize a cost function subject to constraints.
At each sampling instant $k$, MPC solves the Optimal Control Problem:

$$\min_{\{u_0, \ldots, u_{N_c-1}\}} \sum_{i=0}^{N_p - 1} \left\| y_{k+i|k} - r_{k+i} \right\|^2_{W_4} + \left\| \Delta u \right\|^2_{W_3}$$
where $y_{k+i|k}$ is the predicted output $i$ steps ahead, $r_{k+i}$ is the reference trajectory, $W_4$ penalizes tracking error over the prediction horizon $N_p$, $W_3$ penalizes control input increments over the control horizon $N_c$, and $\Delta u$ denotes differences between successive control actions.

For a linear system, the predicted output trajectory over the full prediction horizon can be written compactly as:

$$\mathbf{Y} = \mathcal{O} \mathbf{x}_k + \mathcal{M} \mathbf{U}$$

where $\mathbf{Y} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p r}$ stacks all predicted outputs, $\mathbf{U} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_c m}$ stacks all future inputs, $\mathcal{O}$ is the observability-like matrix encoding free response contributions from the current state, and $\mathcal{M}$ is the lower-triangular Toeplitz matrix encoding input-to-output impulse responses.
Substituting this into the cost function and differentiating yields the unconstrained closed-form optimal gain:

$$\mathbf{U}^* = \underbrace{({\mathcal{M}}^\top W_4 \mathcal{M} + W_3)^{-1} \mathcal{M}^\top W_4}_{\text{gain matrix}} \left( \mathbf{R} - \mathcal{O}\mathbf{x}_k \right)$$

This gain matrix is computed once offline, making the nominal MPC step a simple matrix-vector multiplication.

\subsection{Tube-Based Robust MPC}
Real physical systems are subject to disturbances. Here, process noise $w_k$ enters additively through the input channel, modeling unmodeled forces (friction, measurement-induced torques) so that the true closed-loop dynamics are:

$$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{x}_k + Bu_k + Bw_k$$

A purely nominal MPC ignores $w_k$ and can exhibit large deviations from the desired trajectory. Tube-based MPC addresses this by decomposing the control law into two parts:

$$u_k = \bar{u}_k + K(x_k - \bar{x}_k)$$

where $\bar{x}_k$ is a *nominal* (disturbance-free) state trajectory, $\bar{u}_k$ is the nominal optimal input, and $K(\mathbf{x}_k - \bar{x}_k)$ is an *ancillary* LQR feedback term that drives the actual state back toward the nominal. The nominal system evolves as:

$$\bar{x}_{k+1} = A\bar{x}_k + B\bar{u}_k$$

and the error $e_k = \mathbf{x}_k - \bar{x}_k$ obeys the closed-loop error dynamics:

$$e_{k+1} = (A + BK)\,e_k + Bw_k = A_{\text{cl}}\,e_k + Bw_k$$

For the nominal MPC optimization to remain feasible, the predicted nominal outputs are "tightened" by a margin that accounts for the maximum expected error. Under a worst-case disturbance assumption, the cumulative output deviation after $k$ steps is bounded by the $\ell_1$ gain:

$$h_k = d_{\max} \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \left| C A_{\text{cl}}^j B \right|$$

where $d_{\max}$ is an upper bound on the per-step disturbance magnitude (set here to $3\sigma$, corresponding to the 99th percentile of a zero-mean Gaussian). The set of all possible trajectories consistent with this bound forms a "tube" and guarantees that the actual output remains inside it regardless of disturbance realizations \cite{dey2026adaptivetubempccommon,LANGSON2004125}.

The ancillary feedback gain $K$ is computed by solving the Discrete Algebraic Riccati Equation (DARE):

$$P = A^\top P A - (A^\top P B)(R + B^\top P B)^{-1}(B^\top P A) + Q$$

$$K = -(R + B^\top P B)^{-1}(B^\top P A)$$

with state weight $Q = I$ and input weight $R = 10I$. The chosen weights make the closed-loop matrix $A_{\text{cl}} = A + BK$ strictly stable, ensuring the error tube has finite width.

\subsection{SARAH-M}
SARAH-M \cite{YANG2024122295} is a stochastic optimization algorithm that combines the Stochastic Recursive Gradient Algorithm (SARAH) with a Heavy-Ball momentum term to accelerate convergence on smooth, strongly convex objectives. It operates in an outer-inner loop structure. At the start of each outer epoch $s$, a full gradient $G_0 = \nabla f(w_s)$ is computed over all $n$ samples. A gradient step is then taken to initialize the inner loop: $w_1 = w_s - \eta G_0$.

In the inner loop, at each iteration $k$, a mini-batch $S$ of size $b$ is randomly drawn without replacement. Rather than computing an expensive full gradient, SARAH-M builds a recursive variance-reduced estimator:
$$G_k = \nabla f_S(v_k) - \nabla f_S(v_{k-1}) + G_{k-1}$$
This estimator corrects the stochastic gradient using the difference between successive gradient evaluations on the same batch, reducing variance without requiring a full pass over the data. The parameter update uses the momentum variable $v_k$:
$$w_{k+1} = v_k - \eta G_k$$
$$v_{k+1} = w_{k+1} + \beta(w_{k+1} - w_k)$$
where $\beta$ is the momentum coefficient. The Heavy-Ball term $\beta(w_{k+1} - w_k)$ extrapolates past the current iterate to accelerate progress in persistent descent directions.

The step size is set to $\eta = \frac{1}{2\lambda_{\max}(H)}$, where $H = \mathcal{M}^\top W_4 \mathcal{M} + W_3$ is the Hessian of the MPC cost. This choice guarantees convergence for strongly convex quadratic objectives. After $m$ inner iterations, the outer variable is updated to $w_{s+1} = w_m$ and the next epoch begins.

\section{Main Method}
This section will go over the system model that was implemented, the decomposition of the tube, the robust controller, and the stochastic controller.

\subsection{System Model}
The four-state, single-input, single-output discrete-time model has the form:

$$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{x}_k + Bu_k + Bw_k, \qquad y_k = C\mathbf{x}_k$$

where:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{x}_k = [x_{1,k},\, \dot{x}_{1,k},\, x_{2,k},\, \dot{x}_{2,k}]^\top \in \mathbb{R}^4$ is the full state vector
    \item $u_k \in \mathbb{R}$ is the applied force to mass $m_2$
    \item $y_k \in \mathbb{R}$ is the observed position of mass $m_1$
    \item $w_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ is the scalar process noise with standard deviation $\sigma = 5$, entering through the same input channel as $u_k$
    \item $C = [1, 0, 0, 0]$ extracts the position of $m_1$.
\end{itemize}

The prediction and control horizons are both set to $N_p = N_c = 20$ steps. The desired reference trajectory $r_k$ is a binary pulse signal: value 1 from $k=0$ to $k=99$, value 0 from $k=100$ to $k=199$, and value 1 again from $k=200$ onward, yielding $T=280$ closed-loop steps.

At each time step, predicted outputs over horizon $N_p$ are stacked as $\hat{\mathbf{Y}} = \mathcal{O} \mathbf{x}_k + \mathcal{M} \mathbf{U}$, where $\mathcal{O} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_pr \times n}$ and $\mathcal{M} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_pr \times N_cm}$ are precomputed from the system matrices. The deterministic MPC cost becomes a quadratic in $\mathbf{U}$:
$$\min_{\mathbf{U}} \; \left\|\mathcal{M}\mathbf{U} - (\mathbf{R} - \mathcal{O}\mathbf{x}_k)\right\|^2_{W_4} + \left\|\mathbf{U}\right\|^2_{W_3}$$
with closed-form solution $\mathbf{U}^* = (\mathcal{M}^\top W_4 \mathcal{M} + W_3)^{-1} \mathcal{M}^\top W_4 (\mathbf{R} - \mathcal{O}\mathbf{x}_k)$.

\subsection{Tube Decomposition}
The applied input is split into nominal and feedback parts \cite{LANGSON2004125}:

$$u_k = \bar{u}_k + K(x_k - \bar{x}_k)$$

The LQR gain $K$ is computed via DARE with $Q = I_n$, $R=10I_m$. The nominal state $\bar{x}_k$ evolves noise-free, and the error $e_k = x_k - \bar{x}_k$ follows:

$$e_{k+1} = (A + BK)\,e_k + Bw_k$$

\subsection{Stochastic Tube MPC via SARAH-M}
At each time step, instead of minimizing the nominal cost $$\norm{\mathcal{M}\mathbf{U} - \mathbf{s}}^2_{W_4}$$ (where $\mathbf{s} = \mathbf{R} - \mathcal{O}\bar{x}_k$ encodes the tracking objective), the SARAH-M controller minimizes the expected cost over $n_s$ disturbance scenarios:

$$\min_{\mathbf{U}} \frac{1}{n_s} \sum_{j=1}^{n_s} \left\| \mathcal{M}\mathbf{U} - \mathbf{s}_j \right\|^2_{W_4} + \|\mathbf{U}\|^2_{W_3}$$

where for each $j$, the disturbance-adjusted tracking target is $\mathbf{s}_j = \mathbf{R} - \mathcal{O}\bar{x}_k - D_j$. The term $D_j \in \mathbb{R}^{N_p}$ is the contribution of a simulated disturbance sequence $\{w_{j,t}\}_{t=0}^{N_p - 1}$ propagated through the closed-loop prediction model:

$$D_j^{(t)} = C A_{\text{cl}}^t B w_{j,0} + C A_{\text{cl}}^{t-1} B w_{j,1} + \cdots + C B w_{j,t-1}.$$

This formulation is a direct application of scenario-based stochastic programming: the controller hedges against $n_s=20$ independently drawn futures simultaneously. Because the objective is a quadratic in $\mathbf{U}$ for every scenario, the gradient is:

$$\nabla_\mathbf{U} f_j(\mathbf{U}) = 2\left(H\mathbf{U} - \mathcal{M}^\top W_4 \mathbf{s}_j\right)$$

where $H = \mathcal{M}^\top W_4 \mathcal{M} + W_3$. The full gradient over all scenarios is just the average. SARAH-M is then run for a fixed number of inner steps $m=10$ across $s=5$ outer epochs, with mini-batch size $b=5$ and momentum $\beta=0.1$ After SARAH-M converges, the resulting unconstrained input sequence is projected onto the constraint set: predicted outputs must lie above the desired reference plus the stochastic tightening margin, solved as a quadratic program via OSQP with warm-starting for efficiency.

\subsection{Robust Tube MPC}
The worst-case (robust) tube is computed analytically as described in Section 2.3. The half-width at time step $k$ is:

$$h_k = d_{\max} \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \left| C A_{\text{cl}}^j B \right|, \qquad d_{\max} = 3\sigma$$

This tube grows monotonically with time as disturbance effects accumulate, and is centered on the nominal trajectory produced by running the MPC with $w_k \equiv 0$.

    \label{runtime}

\begin{figure}
    \centering    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{tube_comparison.png}
    \caption{Comparison between Robust MPC and Stochastic MPC Tube Width}
    \label{tube}

The two controllers were simulated on a 300-step pulse reference trajectory (parameters: $N_p = N_c = 20$, $\sigma = 5$, $N_{MC} = 20$ trials for SARAH-M). Figure~\ref{tube} shows the trajectory distributions. The robust worst-case tube (red shaded) is highly conservative. It expands rapidly as disturbance energy accumulates over time. The SARAH-M 95th percentile band is substantially tighter, demonstrating that probabilistic constraint tightening admits less consevative feasbile regions. The stochastic method is able to hug the desired path more closely than the robust method due to the much narrower tube. Both controllers successfully track the desired reference path. Figure~\ref{runtime} shows per-step computation time, comparing the computational complexity of the robust and stochastic methods. The robust controller is fast (closed-form and one OSQP solve). The SARAH-M controller is slower due to the gradient iterations over scenarios, incurring a 3.50 millisecond average overhead. Compared to the robust controller's 2.40 millisecond average overhead, the stochastic's overhead is not significant when considering the 91.5\% efficiency gain from a narrower tube.


\section{Conclusion}
SMPC can be a viable alternative to Robust MPC when safety considerations do not require an aggressive treatment of the worst case scenario.
While Robust MPC provides absolute saftey guarantees, it does so at the expense of the performance of the system.
The core contribution of this study is the empirical validation that higher risk tolerance can be directly translated to operational gain.
In the simulated scenarios, the SMPC controller achieved a 91.9\% gain in the tube width over the robust controller, primarily because the SMPC can avoid low-probability outlier disturbance events that the robust controller is forced to mitigate.
However, the convergence time of the SARAH-M algorithm is 45.4\% slower, which is a noteworthy time penalty for systems that require fast convergence times on the order of milliseconds or faster.
Future work could explore several directions. First, the number of SARAH-M epochs and inner iterations were fixed heuristically; an adaptive stopping criterion based on the gradient norm $\|G_k\|$ could reduce unnecessary computation at steps where the optimizer converges early. Second, the current constraint tightening margin is precomputed offline from the nominal closed-loop dynamics; an online margin that adapts to the observed error covariance could further tighten the tube in low-noise operating conditions. Third, this study used a single-input, single-output system; extending the framework to multi-input, multi-output systems with coupled constraints would test the scalability of the scenario-based approach. Finally, replacing the uniform scenario sampling with importance-weighted sampling concentrated near constraint-active regions could improve both the accuracy of the gradient estimate and the tightness of the resulting feasible set.
